PAKISTAN FAMILY LAWS
Key Provisions for Family Law Matters

MUSLIM FAMILY LAWS ORDINANCE 1961

Section 6: Polygamy - No man during the subsistence of an existing marriage shall contract another marriage except with the previous permission in writing of the Arbitration Council. An application for permission shall be submitted to the Chairman of the Union Council together with the prescribed fee and shall state reasons for the proposed marriage and whether the consent of the existing wife or wives has been obtained. Any man who contracts another marriage without such permission shall pay the entire amount of the dower, whether prompt or deferred, to the existing wife or wives, and on conviction upon complaint shall be punishable with simple imprisonment for a term which may extend to one year or with fine up to five thousand rupees or with both.
[Source: Muslim Family Laws Ordinance 1961]

Section 7: Talaq (Divorce by Husband) - Any man who wishes to divorce his wife shall, as soon as may be after the pronouncement of talaq in any form whatsoever, give the Chairman of the Union Council notice in writing of his having done so. The Chairman shall supply a copy thereof to the wife. Within thirty days of the receipt of notice, the Chairman shall constitute an Arbitration Council for the purpose of bringing about a reconciliation between the parties. If the Arbitration Council fails to achieve reconciliation, the talaq shall not be effective until the expiration of ninety days from the date on which notice was delivered to the Chairman.
[Source: Muslim Family Laws Ordinance 1961]

Section 8: Dissolution of Marriage (Khula) - A Muslim woman may seek dissolution of her marriage through the court by filing a suit for Khula. The woman must return the dower (Mehr) or any other benefit received from the husband. The court shall attempt reconciliation and if it fails, the court shall pass a decree for dissolution. Khula is the right of the wife to seek divorce when she finds it impossible to live within the limits prescribed by Allah.
[Source: Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act 1939]

Section 9: Dower (Mehr) - Dower is a sum of money or other property which the wife is entitled to receive from the husband in consideration of the marriage. It may be prompt (payable on demand) or deferred (payable on dissolution of marriage or death of husband). If the amount of dower is not fixed, the wife is entitled to proper dower based on the social status and financial position of the husband.
[Source: Muslim Family Laws Ordinance 1961]

Section 10: Maintenance (Nafaqah) - A husband is bound to maintain his wife during the subsistence of the marriage and during the period of iddat after divorce. Maintenance includes food, clothing, and lodging according to the husband's means and social status. A wife who is not maintained by her husband may apply to the Family Court for an order of maintenance. The court may order the husband to pay maintenance as it deems fit.
[Source: West Pakistan Family Courts Act 1964]

WEST PAKISTAN FAMILY COURTS ACT 1964

Section 5: Jurisdiction - Family Courts shall have exclusive jurisdiction to adjudicate upon matters relating to dissolution of marriage, dower, maintenance, restitution of conjugal rights, custody of children (Hizanat), guardianship, jactitation of marriage, and dowry.
[Source: West Pakistan Family Courts Act 1964]

Section 10: Pre-trial Reconciliation - On the institution of a suit, the Family Court shall fix a date for pre-trial reconciliation proceedings. The Court shall make every effort to bring about reconciliation and settlement between the parties. If reconciliation fails, the Court shall proceed with the suit.
[Source: West Pakistan Family Courts Act 1964]

CHILD CUSTODY (HIZANAT)

Under Hanafi law, the mother has the right of custody (hizanat) of a male child until the age of seven years and a female child until she attains puberty. After these ages, the father has the right of custody. However, the paramount consideration in all custody matters is the welfare and best interest of the child. The court may deviate from these rules if the welfare of the child so requires.
[Source: Guardian and Wards Act 1890]

GUARDIANSHIP

The father is the natural guardian of the person and property of his minor children. After the father, guardianship passes to the father's father (paternal grandfather). The court may appoint any person as guardian if it considers it for the welfare of the minor. A guardian cannot be appointed without the prior consent of the court.
[Source: Guardian and Wards Act 1890]

IDDAT PERIOD

After divorce, a Muslim woman must observe Iddat for a period of three menstrual cycles or three lunar months if she does not menstruate. If the woman is pregnant, the Iddat extends until the delivery of the child. During the Iddat period, the husband is obligated to provide maintenance to the divorced wife. The wife cannot remarry during the Iddat period.
[Source: Muslim Family Laws Ordinance 1961]

DOWRY AND BRIDAL GIFTS (RESTRICTION) ACT 1976

Section 3: No person shall give or take or abet the giving or taking of dowry exceeding the value prescribed by law. The total value of dowry shall not exceed five thousand rupees. Any person who gives or takes dowry in excess of this limit shall be punishable with imprisonment for a term not exceeding six months or with a fine or with both.
[Source: Dowry and Bridal Gifts (Restriction) Act 1976]

DOMESTIC VIOLENCE (PREVENTION AND PROTECTION) ACT 2012

Section 4: A woman or child may file a complaint of domestic violence before a court having jurisdiction. Domestic violence includes physical abuse, sexual abuse, psychological abuse, economic abuse, stalking, or cybercrime. The court may pass a protection order restraining the defendant from committing any act of domestic violence. Violation of a protection order is punishable with imprisonment up to one year or fine up to one hundred thousand rupees.
[Source: Domestic Violence (Prevention and Protection) Act 2012]

NIKAH (MARRIAGE CONTRACT)

A valid Muslim marriage (Nikah) requires offer (Ijab) and acceptance (Qubool) in the presence of at least two male witnesses or one male and two female witnesses. The bride's consent is essential and must be freely given. Marriage must be registered with the Nikah Registrar within the prescribed time. Failure to register a marriage is an offense punishable with fine or imprisonment.
[Source: Muslim Family Laws Ordinance 1961]
